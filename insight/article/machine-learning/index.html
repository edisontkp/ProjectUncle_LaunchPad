LYNK Expert Insight: Maching Learning

23/03/2017

Author: Eric Tsang

Position: LYNK CTO

Quick Read
  • Humans and machines face a steep learning curve
  • Despite recent headlines, Machine Learning will help more workers than it will replace
  • Consulting firms and hedge funds will need to change the way they recruit and operate
  • Soon we’ll all be able to prepare for our jobs like a pilot using a simulator

Machine Learning - more to learn

When it comes to implementing Machine Learning systems, CTOs and programmers often have amazing subject knowledge but are typically self-taught. According to Radim Řehůřek, many have learned to get the job done with just one tool – “they have a hammer and everything looks like a nail”. He routinely finds this approach leads to huge costs and productivity issues given the man-hours and servers required.

However, it’s not just humans but also machines that are facing a steep learning curve. Lorien Pratt explains that today’s technology “is getting really good at doing what ears and eyes do – like recognizing that’s a cat or a dog, or ‘I know roughly what you said or what you wrote’. However, it’s not yet so good at reasoning with that information.”

FEATURED EXPERTS

Lorien Pratt PhD is the Co-founder of Quantellia, the Silicon Valley headquartered machine learning and decision intelligence solutions provider. Lorien is a machine learning pioneer with 30+ years’ experience working with NASA among others.

Tom Livne is the Founder and CEO of VerbIT.ai, a revolutionary cloud-based speech recognition technology which relies on the latest advances in deep learning neural networks. Tom is a serial entrepreneur and is working to develop the world’s most efficient and cost effective transcription service.

Radim Řehůřek PhD is the Director of RARE Technologies, a machine learning and data mining specialist consultancy. Currently based in South Korea, Radim has over 10 years’ experience consulting in cutting edge data mining, text analytics, NLP, big data and search.

Will we be replaced by robots?

Debate around the extent to which human intelligence could be replaced by artificial intelligence in the workplace has gathered pace in recent weeks. Lorien suggests that “just like the invention of automobiles reduced the need for blacksmiths, AI will decimate certain jobs, for example those in manufacturing and marketing”. Similarly, Radim predicts that while roles grounded in human interaction and ‘the physical’ are here to stay and will in fact become increasingly valuable, functions involving basic digital data wrangling will be commoditized away by computers.

Yet, whilst Machine Learning will likely impact almost every imaginable field, he believes that it will ultimately help more workers than it will replace. For Lorien, the big mistake we’re currently making is looking at things from a solely-autonomous perspective - people will still be involved, even if their job titles change. She says that "the use cases that are generating the most revenue today are those where the machine works alone. That's going to change soon, with AI increasingly working hand-in-hand with humans to help with the most important decisions".

From a transcription perspective, Tom Livne doesn’t foresee mass redundancies in the next 10 years, as AI is not yet capable of 99 percent perfection. But even as his system matches, or even exceeds, human capabilities, he expects “the pie to grow and not to be cut smaller”. Once we come to the stage where marketers, universities and others only need to pay 20c, rather than $1 per minute for transcription, the volume of work and applications will inevitably grow.

For example, Tom foresees an abundance of new opportunities for management consultancies and organizations that frequently employ large teams that typically work in different languages across numerous markets. At the same time, Radim expects that AI specialists will increasingly challenge the traditional consulting model because of their ability to look at the data and experiment at a much deeper level.

What next?

All three experts agree that the marketing and advertising industries, spearheaded by the likes of Google and Amazon, are still years ahead of most other industries. However, Lorien thinks the financial services industry, particularly in the U.S., has positioned itself firmly next in line.

Some of her recent work has focused on developing systems for insurers and lenders who are significantly investing in the mitigation of the effect of climate change and related catastrophes. Simultaneously, she’s also observed that hedge funds and quant investors are currently quietly hiring talent in this space.

For Radim, Unsupervised Learning represents the next big step as it will require less data preparation by humans. He explains that there are already several algorithms being developed “and that there is serious money to be made that isn’t being tapped.” On top of reducing costs, this approach will be invaluable given the sheer volume of raw and unstructured data that companies are typically working with.

Similarly for transcription, the systems being developed by Verbit.ai to adapt to specific niches, whilst also maintaining high accuracy and minimizing human involvement, are starting to capture attention. From the instant captioning of videos on Facebook to online conferencing and broker calls, it will soon be possible to achieve previously unimaginable accuracy almost instantaneously, and at a fraction of the cost.

According to Lorien, the next big step for AI is Decision Intelligence (DI). She says that "tomorrow's AI systems will be able to answer the question 'if I take this action today, what will be the outcome tomorrow?'". Pratt explains that being able to answer this question is something that most AI systems today can't do, but that it's a natural extension. "In DI, the AI system can learn each link in a causal chain from the initial action to the outcomes". She goes on to say that "to get this right will require Intelligence Augmentation where humans provide the knowledge of the world as a causal system and AI learns exactly how each link in the chain operates".

She believes this is what has been missing from AI and “is going to explode within the next two years”. Just like a trainee pilot, professionals from any number of industries will soon start to benefit from a simulator experience, allowing them to prepare and predict outcomes before making a decision.
